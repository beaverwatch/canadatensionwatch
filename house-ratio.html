📈 The Rich-Poor Gap — How Housing Creates It
🔴 Housing is the #1 driver of wealth inequality in Canada
When homes cost more than 9x income, wealth becomes permanently locked in the hands of those who already own property.

The math is brutal:
→ A Toronto homeowner who bought in 2010 for $500K now holds an asset worth $1.1M — a $600K gain they did nothing to earn.
→ A renter who earned the same salary over the same period has zero housing wealth.

This creates a permanent two-tier Canada:
→ Owners: wealth compounds through real estate inflation regardless of income
→ Renters: income stagnates, rent rises, wealth accumulation becomes impossible

Result: The richest 20% of Canadians own 67% of all real estate wealth. The bottom 40% own less than 3%. This gap is accelerating — not stabilizing. Source: Statistics Canada Survey of Financial Security 2025.

🔴 Long-Term Cascade — 5 to 20 years
3–5
years
Young talent exodus accelerates
When housing is unaffordable, young educated Canadians leave for lower-cost countries. Already documented: Canada lost a record number of 25–34 year olds to emigration in 2024. Source: StatCan.
5–10
years
Birth rate continues to fall
Housing cost is the #1 reason cited by Canadians for having fewer children than desired. At >9x income ratio, family formation becomes financially impossible for the median earner. Source: Angus Reid 2024.
5–15
years
Permanent wealth stratification
A generation that cannot buy property will never accumulate the same wealth as their parents. This creates a hereditary wealth class — those who inherited property — and a permanent renter class. This is the end of social mobility as Canada has known it.
10–20
years
Political rupture
Every society where housing has been inaccessible for more than 10 years has seen a major political rupture. New Zealand 2023, UK 2024: housing affordability was the primary driver of government change. Canada is 3–5 years behind on this curve.
🔗 Why the ratio keeps rising
01
Supply constraint — Canada builds fewer homes per capita than any other G7 country. Zoning laws, NIMBYism and construction costs keep supply chronically below demand.
02
Investment demand — Canadian homes are treated as investment vehicles, not shelter. Institutional investors, REITs and foreign buyers compete with first-time buyers, driving prices beyond what wages can sustain.
03
Immigration without housing — Canada adds 400,000+ immigrants per year to a housing stock that grows by 200,000 units. The structural gap compounds every year.
04
Political inaction — homeowners vote more than renters. Politicians whose voters own property have no incentive to reduce home values. This structural conflict of interest has prevented meaningful reform for 20 years.

Methodology
Score based on national average price-to-income ratio. Ratio 3x = 0.0. Ratio 5x = 0.30 (historical warning threshold). Ratio 7x = 0.60. Ratio 9x = 0.80. Ratio 12x+ = 1.0. Current national average >9x = score 0.80 — Critical. Vancouver and Toronto individually would score 0.95+.
